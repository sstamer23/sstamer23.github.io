About

Exoplanets, education, and science communication

I am currently a Ph.D. student in Physics with an Astrophysics Concentration at the University of New Mexico. I completed my Bachelor of Science in Astronomy and Physics at the University of Arizona, with Honors in Astronomy, in May 2024.

My current research focuses on exoplanet atmospheres. I have also worked on astronomy education and science literacy, including projects on student reasoning in astrobiology writing, scalable grading in MOOCs, and combating science misinformation online.

Outside of classes and research, I enjoy playing percussion, reading, curling, and crocheting.

Current focus

  • Analyzing JWST transmission spectrum data from the NIRSpec instrument for the hot Neptune desert planet LTT 9779b.
  • Working toward a first-author paper on LTT 9779b.
  • Building on past research in exoplanet characterization, post-habitable planets, and astronomy education.

Recent support

  • New Mexico Space Grant Graduate Research Fellowship, 2025–2026.
  • William G. Larsen, PhD, Memorial Award for Best Teaching Assistant, 2025–2026.
Research

Selected work

Exoplanet atmospheres, future missions, rocky planet composition, and astronomy education research.

Analyzing JWST Observations of LTT 9779b

My pre-candidacy project at UNM leads the analysis of JWST/NIRSpec transmission spectrum data for LTT 9779b, an exoplanet in the hot Neptune desert. This project is partially funded by a New Mexico Space Grant Graduate Research Fellowship, and I am working on a first-author paper.

  • JWST
  • NIRSpec
  • Exoplanet atmospheres

Characterizing Post-Habitable Exoplanets with HWO

At the University of Arizona's Lunar and Planetary Laboratory, I developed atmospheric retrieval models to explore what kinds of spectral data the future Habitable Worlds Observatory would need to identify Venus-like exoplanets.

  • HWO
  • Atmospheric retrievals
  • Venus analogs

Rocky Exoplanets, Host Stars, and Education Research

Through an NSF REU at UNM, I calculated iron-to-silicate mass fractions for stars and planets from stellar spectra and found a weaker composition trend than earlier work. My education research includes an honors thesis on student reasoning in astrobiology MOOC writing, scalable grading of student writing, and research on science misinformation online.

  • Rocky exoplanets
  • Stellar composition
  • Science education

Outreach

Teaching, outreach, and community

Supporting students and sharing astronomy beyond the classroom.

Teaching

At UNM, I was a teaching assistant for Introductory Astronomy Lab for three semesters and Physics of Music for one semester. I aim to build an approachable, supportive classroom environment where students feel comfortable asking questions and working through challenging material.

Student evaluations have highlighted my patience, clarity, step-by-step explanations, prompt feedback, and emphasis on interactive, hands-on learning. For my work as a TA, I received the 2025–2026 William G. Larsen, PhD, Memorial Award for Best Teaching Assistant.

Outreach and service

At UNM, I volunteer as a telescope operator for Campus Observatory Friday night open houses. I have also judged high school and undergraduate research projects and given outreach talks to local elementary students and at Space Center Houston during graduate school.

At the University of Arizona, I worked as a Planetarium Operator and Science Center Interpreter at Flandrau Science Center and Planetarium, the only planetarium in Southern Arizona.

Diversity, equity, and inclusion

I have been involved in leadership roles in the Physics and Astronomy Graduate Student Association and Women in Physics, Astronomy, and Optics. I am the current Meeting Manager for WiPAO and the current GSA Co-President.

During my final two years at the University of Arizona, I was a peer leader for the TIMESTEP program, sitting on panels to share my experiences and promote diversity and inclusion in astrophysics.
